Quick Notes

Short revision points based only on your uploaded Society and Economy notes.

Society

  • Sociological imagination means viewing personal experiences through wider social forces.
  • Society is an autonomous grouping with common territory, common culture, social interaction, and interdependent roles.
  • Know the 7 features of society: size, culture, territory, belonging, origin/destiny, language, autonomy/interdependence.
  • Arawaks were linked to larger islands and seen as more peaceful; Caribs lived on smaller islands and resisted more fiercely.
  • Caribbean history is often split into indigenous peoples, Spaniards, European competition, slavery/plantation, and indentureship.
  • Plantation Society Theory links Caribbean structure to mono-crop production, race stratification, peasant marginalisation, and dependency.
  • Plural Society Theory says groups keep separate institutions and mainly meet in the marketplace.
  • Creole Society Theory focuses on cultural fusion, especially European and African interaction, through acculturation, interculturation, and syncretism.
  • Absolute poverty is failure to meet basic survival needs; relative poverty is deprivation compared with wider social standards.
  • Crime breaks formal law; deviance breaks social norms. All crime is deviant, but not all deviance is crime.

Economy

  • Economics examines problems and decisions of economic agents from a social perspective.
  • Microeconomics studies consumers and producers; macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole.
  • Micro goals: efficiency, equity, and freedom of choice.
  • Macroeconomic functions include monetary policy, fiscal policy, and trade policy.
  • Macroeconomic goals include high employment, price-level stability, and economic growth.
  • The Plantation Economy School argues Caribbean underdevelopment is rooted in plantation structures tied to metropolitan capitalism.
  • Best and Levitt identify conquest, settlement, and exploitation hinterlands; the Caribbean fits exploitation hinterlands.
  • Globalisation reduces the significance of geographic, economic, and cultural boundaries for flows of capital, goods, people, and ideas.
  • Post-1990s globalisation features revolutionary technologies, new institutional actors, and liberalisation/neo-liberalism.
  • FDI can bring capital, technology, employment, and tax revenue, but may also threaten sovereignty, displace local firms, and cause profit repatriation.
